# Cognitive Skills

### Systems Thinking

Thinking as a whole/big picture, how individual components within a bounded system interact with one another to form a whole. Instead of looking at isolated events, it focuses on cycles, feedback loops, and interdependencies.

* Example: When a city experiences heavy traffic, a systems thinker doesn't just suggest widening one road. They look at how road expansion might encourage more people to drive (induced demand), how it affects public transit usage, and how it impacts housing density in the suburbs.

### Abstract Thinking

The capacity to understand concepts that are not physically present or concrete. It involves using symbols, metaphors, and generalizations to understand complex ideas or patterns that exist beyond the "here and now."

* Example: Understanding the concept of "Justice" or "Freedom." You cannot touch "Justice," but you can identify its presence or absence across different laws, social interactions, and historical events by recognizing the underlying pattern.

### Metacognition

"thinking about thinking." It is the awareness and understanding of one's own thought processes, including the ability to monitor, control, and plan how you learn or solve a problem.

* Example: A student realizes they are struggling to understand a chemistry chapter. Instead of just re-reading the same page, they stop and ask themselves, "Why am I confused? Is it the vocabulary or the math?" They then decide to watch a video tutorial to approach the problem differently.

### Spatial Awareness

The organized knowledge of objects in relation to oneself and in relation to other objects. It involves understanding depth, distance, and how shapes fit together or move through space.

* Example: Parallel parking a car. You must mentally calculate the size of your vehicle, the size of the empty spot, and how the angle of your steering wheel will change the car's position relative to the curb and the cars behind you.

### Reverse Engineering

The process of deconstructing a finished product, system, or idea to understand its internal structure, function, and design logic, often to recreate it or improve upon it.

* Example: A chef tastes a complex sauce at a famous restaurant and tries to identify the individual spices, the base fats used, and the cooking technique (e.g., reduction vs. thickening) just by analyzing the final flavor profile and texture.

### Domain-Driven Architectural Thinking

You start with the business reality, but you immediately move to the "Abstract" level to identify the technical characteristics (scalability, security, etc.) that will dictate the system's success or failure.
